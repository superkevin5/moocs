Table 5.0 Results of reliability test (Cronbach’s alpha =.9) suggests a strong internal correlation between the 9 survey items in the course satisfaction block.

Cronbach’s alpha is a measure of internal consistency. A high Cronbach’s alpha means the tested survey items are uni-dimensional. In practice, the nine questions may mean ‘overall satisfaction’ to respondents. Factor analysis is usually performed to test further.

Most of the correlation coefficient values are > .30, suggesting data suitable for factor analysis.

Table 3.4 Factor correlation

Factor correlation around .30 suggests the two factors have low correlation coefficients. Therefore Oblimin rotation is selected. And the SPSS outcomes comprise two factor tables – pattern and structure (table 3.6 and table 3.7). 

 

 

 

 

Table 3.5 Communalities values of all items

Legend

All survey items have relatively high loading values (>.5), which suggest they all fit in the two factors’ model.

As the course satisfaction block is designed that all questions contribute to the variance of a single concept of ‘overall course satisfaction’, it is not surprising that factor analysis returns the results suggesting all survey items load onto one factor.

 

A forced factor analysis is run to bring out the structure of the factor(s) to a more interpretable extent. It forced to extract two factors and applied a rotation technique was selected. (Oblimin was selected due to the relatively low correlation between factors.)

 

Satisfied course quality, the met expectations, encouraged interest, and improved skills for career are the top four items in results of FA tests without rotation. Learners seem to appreciate the engaging style of the presented course materials (.823~.874), and the use of examples (including illustrations and real-world cases) (.794~.856) to enhance their learning, as opposed to quizzes (.587~.721) and forums (.015~ .433).

‘Course satisfaction through effective learning by engaging with the course’ is abstracted and proposed to be the factor.

Forums were designed primarily for learners to interact with each other. However, the fact that the forum is seen less contributory to overall satisfaction is congruent with what (Swan, 2011) concluded that engaging with instructors through the course have a much larger effect on satisfaction and learners’ perceived learning, than engaging with peers.

INTROSE IVQ N = 17856

Figure 1. Respondents’ Geo distribution

1.      Learners’ ratings of ‘I find this lecture useful’.

2.      Learners’ ratings of ‘I understand the content of this lecture’.

3.      Learners’ ratings of ‘I would like to explore other modules of this course’.

Figure 2. Overview

In the responses of all three questions, the mean ratings across all module videos are flat, regardless to the length of a video, or the week of the video.

One-way ANOVA is performed to test if lecture videos across all modules make a significant difference between the mean ratings of modules in Q1, Q2 and Q3.

Levene’s homogeneity test suggests there is a significant difference between mean ratings in each of Q1 and Q2 across modules. There is NOT a significant difference between mean ratings in Q3. The homogeneous subsets in table 1 adhere to the results of the test.

Table 1. Homogeneous subsets

Results of One-way ANOVA suggest lecture videos across modules a significant factor that have resulted in the variance in learners’ level of agreement (in form of their ratings) to item ‘I find this lecture useful’ and to item ‘I understand the content of this lecture’. But how likely would learners explore other modules in this course is not significantly influenced by the lecture videos across modules (as a factor). Table 2 shows the ANOVA statistics.

Table 2. ANOVA results of three questions assuming lecture videos across all modules a factor

 

Another one way ANOVA is performed to test if whether in signature track or not also a factor resulting in the variance in learners’ ratings.

A p = 0.000 in all three questions indicates whether learners are in signature track is also a factor that has made a significant difference between their ratings.

A third factor of video length in second is tested in one-way ANOVA to test if different video lengths makes ratings significantly different (results in table 3).

Table 3a test of model grouping factors of lecture videos and signature-track

 

Figure 3.1 overview of magnitude difference in signature track factor across all module lecture videos

Estimated marginal means measure if the magnitude of difference in (non)-signature track is equal across all modules.

 

In signature track learners in general gave higher rates to all three items across modules, compared to the ones given by non-signature track learners.

 

In Q1, the signature track factor has less effect on learners’ view of the lecture videos as the course moves toward later weeks. In Q2, the factor’s effect is generally the same across all modules, and whether in signature track does not seem to be a factor largely differentiating the ratings of the two groups. In Q3, across all modules, the magnitude of different in (non)-signature track is generally equal, and in signature track learners have presented higher rates.

 

However, the model of having lecture videos across modules and whether in signature track or not have made non-significant difference to learners’ ratings across three questions (Table 3)

 

Table 3. ANOVA results of testing the factor of video length

Figure 3.2 overview of magnitude difference of signature track factor across all video lengths

 

In signature track learners in general gave higher rates to all three items across all lecture video lengths, as compared to the ratings given by non-signature track learners.

 

Signature track does not seem to be a factor that significantly differentiates the ratings of the two learner groups across all video lengths. The magnitude difference of this factor is in general equal in Q2 and Q3. The factor seems to have a bigger influence on differentiating two groups’ ratings to ‘I found the lecture video useful’ (Q1) in videos of which lengths are between 8 to 11 minutes or 480 to 692 seconds.

 

Chi-square test found significant differences in learners’ ratings between each pair of the three questions. Phi and Cramer’s V both less than 0.05 between Q1 and Q3, Q2 and Q3 indicate there is a significant difference between learners’ ratings in each of the three questions.

D. Cross-comparison between questions in Pre- and post- surveys

There are 584 respondents who filled in both pre-course survey and post-course survey. Among the 584, 457 of them (78.2%) are in non-signature track, and 127 (21.8%) are in signature track.

Out of the 524 respondents who indicated they wanted to earn a certificate/statement of accomplishment (Q50) in the pre-course survey, 401 of them were from the NST cohort, 123 from the ST cohort. There is one respondent who was in signature track indicated that he/she did not want to earn a certificate/statement of accomplishment.
